# 13-threat-matrix.md
EventDB Core - Threat Matrix
Version: 0.1
Status: Draft
Change Log Reference: 00-admin/PROJECT_MEMORY.md

EventDB Core provides integrity-oriented protection through canonical hashing, hash chaining, signature verification, and Seal continuity checks. These controls are designed to make unauthorized history changes detectable and attributable within defined governance boundaries. The model does not guarantee factual correctness of Event payloads, legal validity, or complete prevention of insider misuse. Residual risk remains when key governance fails, verification is not executed, or policy enforcement is weak. This matrix defines major integrity threats, expected impact, primary mitigation mechanism, and remaining exposure after controls are applied.

| Threat | Attack Vector | Impact | Mitigation Mechanism | Residual Risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Event modification | Direct alteration of stored Event fields after issuance. | Hash mismatch, signature invalidation, and integrity dispute for affected Chain segment. | Canonical hash coverage over required fields; signature verification against canonical input; deterministic chain re-verification. | If compromised key signs malicious content at issuance, payload can remain invalid in business terms while still cryptographically consistent. |
| Event deletion | Removal of one or more historical Events from Chain storage. | Sequence discontinuity and broken continuity for downstream verification. | Sequence monotonicity checks; `prev_hash` linkage validation; Seal window commitment mismatch detection. | Delayed detection if verification cadence is infrequent or scope is partial. |
| Event insertion between historical records | Injection of synthetic Event into prior sequence position. | Reordering/insertion inconsistency and invalid historical continuity. | Deterministic sequence validation; `prev_hash` chain recomputation; Seal commitment recomputation for impacted windows. | Coordinated rewrite attempts in a fully compromised domain may delay discovery until independent verification is performed. |
| Seal omission | Expected Seal is not produced for a closed window. | Reduced checkpoint visibility and wider replay scope for integrity review. | Deterministic window policy; expected Seal cadence monitoring; `prev_seal_hash` continuity checks. | Omission may persist if governance monitoring is weak or alerting is not enforced. |
| Snapshot manipulation | Tampered or stale Snapshot is used as if authoritative. | Incorrect derived state and misleading operational reads. | Snapshot-to-Chain recomputation checks; requirement that Snapshot derives from verified Chain/Seal basis; fallback to underlying artifacts. | If operators trust Snapshot without periodic re-verification, detection latency increases. |
| Key compromise | Unauthorized actor obtains Account signing key and issues valid signatures. | Fraudulent but cryptographically valid Event/Seal issuance under compromised identity. | Signature attribution to `account_id`; key rotation/revocation policy; compromise window re-verification and governance escalation. | Cryptography cannot distinguish authorized vs unauthorized use of the same compromised key. |
| Anchor replay attack | Reuse of previously valid Anchor evidence to imply freshness or wrong checkpoint context. | False confidence in publication recency or checkpoint state. | Anchor verification binds commitment to specific Chain and Seal context; external timestamp comparison; checkpoint identity checks. | Replay can still mislead consumers that skip context binding and policy-based freshness checks. |
| Timestamp falsification | Manipulation of local Event/Seal time fields or inconsistent time source usage. | Distorted chronology claims and audit interpretation errors. | Deterministic format validation; sequence/linkage verification independent of local timestamp claims; optional external Anchor timestamp correlation. | Time claims remain partially policy-dependent; external timestamp does not validate business truth or legal effect. |
